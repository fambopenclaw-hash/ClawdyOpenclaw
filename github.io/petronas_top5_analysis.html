Criterion 5 — H2S Risk by Reservoir

🔴 High H2S Risk Reservoirs HIGH

Nahr Umar, Zubair, Yamama, Mishrif, Maudud, Ratawi, Lower Fars
Carbonate formations (Cretaceous) in southern Iraq are thermally mature with significant quantities of H₂S from TSR (Thermic Sulfate Reduction). H2S can range 50–500+ ppm in produced gas, >1% in some well fluids. Requires: CRA/L80/13Cr tubulars, EHSS sour service protocols, gas dehydration, dedicated H2S detection + breathing apparatus on every well location. OPEX uplift ~15–25% vs. sweet fields.
Affected: Luhais, Subba, Abu Amood, Nasiriyah, Eridu, Noor, Amara, Ballad, Tikrit (Zubair/Mishrif), Khashab (Lower Fars)

🟡 Moderate H2S Risk Reservoirs MODERATE

Jeribe, Euphrates, Kurra Chine, Kaista
Mixed clastic/carbonate; H2S generation possible but typically <50 ppm in produced gas. Standard E&P handling with periodic monitoring sufficient. OPEX uplift ~5–10%.
Affected: Noor (Jeribe), Khanuqah (Euphrates), Kumait, Anah (Kurra Chine), Haifa (Kaista), As-Safa (Kaista)

🟢 Low H2S Risk Reservoirs LOW

Khabour (Devonian sandstone)
Clastic sandstone; H2S generated primarily from bacterial sulfate reduction near surface. Typically <10 ppm. Simplest EHS protocols. Lowest OPEX for gas processing. Akkas, Akashat, Rawa, Al-Khaleesa, Rutba produce from Khabour and analogues — most "petronas-friendly" from an H2S standpoint.
Affected: Akkas, Akashat, Rawa, Rutba, Al-Khaleesa, As-Safa (Khabour zone), Al-Anz

Nasiriyah Field — The Flagship Candidate

SCORE 22/25

Why Nasiriyah scores highest across the board:

HC Evacuation (5/5): Connected to existing pipeline infrastructure (265 km from nearest processing, per spreadsheet). TOC is already producing 50–60 kbd from this field — the offtake chain is proven and operational. No new major pipeline required for initial development phases. Petronas can tie into existing Basrah export infrastructure immediately.

Security (5/5): Located in Thi Qar, southern Iraq — firmly in government-controlled territory with strong MoO/TOC presence. International IOC operating environment well-established. No ISIS presence. Low personal security risk for expatriate staff. This is Petronas' most important threshold — southern IOC fields are the safest operational environment in Iraq.

Future Energy Demand (5/5): Feb 2026 MOU signed between MoO + TOC + Chevron. This is a major signal — Chevron (a supermajor) has independently assessed Nasiriyah as commercially viable at 250 kbd PPT. The fact that Chevron has committed to this field validates its economics without Petronas needing to conduct independent market analysis. Petronas could potentially joint-operate alongside Chevron or partner in a different capacity. Iraq's ambition to reach 6+ mbpd by 2030 gives national backing. Nasiriyah's 250 kbd target aligns with Basrah export expansion.

HC Volume (5/5): Nasiriyah holds 16,000 MMstb STOIIP and 4,900 MMboe reserves — the largest in the portfolio by reserves, and a genuine giant field. At 250 kbd PPT, this field has a 30+ year production life. For Petronas Carigali, this is a transformative reserve addition that moves the needle on corporate reserves replacement.

H2S Risk (2/5): This is Nasiriyah's main weakness. Reservoirs include Mishrif, Nahr Umar, Yamama, and Zubair — all high-H2S carbonate formations. H2S levels can exceed 100 ppm in the Mishrif and Yamama. However, this is manageable with proper well integrity management, CRA tubulars, and gas dehydration. Many IOCs operate sour fields globally. The sour handling requirement adds perhaps 15–20% to development CAPEX but does not disqualify the opportunity. Petronas has sour field experience in Malaysia (e.g., Sokong, Pulai) and gas stewardship through Petronas Carigali's portfolio. The mitigation is known and tested.

Key Recommendation: Pursue a farm-in or joint-study agreement with TOC. Nasiriyah is Petronas' best single entry point into Iraq — giant volume, existing infrastructure, Chevron-validation, and a stable operating environment. H2S is manageable with sour-field expertise Petronas already has.

Akkas Gas Field — Iraq Gas-Power Strategic Fit

SCORE 20/25

Why Akkas is a different kind of opportunity — a gas/power play:

HC Evacuation (4/5): Akkas is unique in this portfolio — it has a direct contractual gas sales agreement with the Akkas Power Plant. The gas doesn't need a long-distance export pipeline; it flows from wellhead to power plant. This is a fixed, government-backed offtake contract — lowest market price risk of any gas asset here. The 25.4 km pipeline distance is short. The only weakness is that if gas demand at the power plant is capped, growth is limited until a new buyer is found.

Security (2/5): This is Akkas' main vulnerability. ISIS controlled Anbar from 2014–2017 and while the province has been reclaimed, the security environment remains more complex than southern Iraq. The area is sparsely populated desert, border proximity to Syria creates cross-border risk, and land access requires security protocols. However: MDOC and SLB have maintained operations at Akkas throughout — this demonstrates that安全生产 is achievable. SLB's willingness to be the operator signals they have internal security assessments that support continued operations.

Future Energy Demand (5/5): This is Akkas' strongest card. Iraq has a structural gas deficit — the country imports gas from Iran and suffers chronic power shortages despite being an oil producer. Domestic gas demand for power generation is growing at 5–8% p.a. A gas field connected to a power plant is Iraq's highest-priority National Development Plan asset. Government support for Akkas expansion is strong. Petronas Gas is well-positioned to be the partner of choice for a gas-to-power story.

HC Volume (4/5): 4,500 Bscf GIIP and 1,245 MMboe reserves (gas). The PPT target of 400 MMscfd (vs. current 40 MMscfd) implies a 10x production ramp — enormous growth headroom. At full PPT, Akkas would become a major gas supply source, potentially feeding a regional gas grid or additional power plants. Anah's large GIIP (7,411 Bscf) could tie into the Akkas processing hub in future — creating a northern gas province anchored by Akkas.

H2S Risk (5/5): Khabour sandstone is the lowest-H2S reservoir system in the portfolio. Bacterial sulfate reduction (BSR) in clastic rocks generates minimal H2S — typically <10 ppm. This is the cleanest gas in the Iraq portfolio. For Petronas, this means: lower CAPEX (no sour-service tubulars), lower OPEX (no continuous H2S monitoring infrastructure), and simpler EHS protocols. This is operationally the cleanest entry point in Iraq.

Key Recommendation: Akkas is the ideal gas entry for Petronas — suited for Petronas Carigali's gas new ventures mandate. The gas-to-power model aligns with Petronas' integrated gas value chain strategy. The security risk, while real, is manageable with a strong in-country security partner (MDOC/SLB already on ground). If Nasiriyah is the oil priority, Akkas is the gas priority. Consider both simultaneously.

This is a strategic infrastructure bet — the kind of position Petronas' large-project execution capability is designed for.

Security (2/5): Anbar Province — same elevated security environment as Akkas. Southern Anbar, ~20 km from Iraq–Saudi border, is remote and sparsely populated. Border security concerns. However, the area is not near active ISIS strongholds and the Jordan border region has historically been more stable than western Anbar (Akkas/Rutba).

Future Energy Demand (4/5): The Jordan export route is Haifa's biggest demand hook. Jordan's energy demand is growing and the country has been seeking gas supply diversity. The Iraq–Jordan pipeline has been discussed at government level for years. If it materialises, Haifa (together with As-Safa) becomes a cornerstone supplier for a new cross-border gas export value chain. This aligns with Petronas' LNG and gas trading footprint.

HC Volume (4/5): Haifa has the largest oil STOIIP in the entire open block portfolio — 3,583 MMstb and 1,075 MMboe oil reserves, plus 98 Bscf gas. This is not a small exploration gamble — the STOIIP is comparable to a discovered field. The Kaista reservoir is moderate-H2S risk (vs. the high-H2S Mishrif/Nahr Umar systems). Reserves-wise, this is a large oil accumulation with a gas cap — analogous to a medium-scale Khuff or Arab Formation oil field.

H2S Risk (4/5): Kaista reservoir is a clastic/carbonate mix with moderate H2S potential — lower than the Mishrif/Nahr Umar Yamama system in the south. OPEX uplift for H2S handling is moderate (10–15%), not the 20–25% seen in the high-sour fields.

Key Recommendation: Haifa is the exploration-stage entry that best balances volume, demand upside, and manageable risk. Petronas should seek an Exploration Period + Production Sharing Agreement with MDOC, committing to a 2–3 well exploration programme. The large STOIIP de-risks the exploration well outcome — even at conservative recovery factors, Haifa could deliver 200–500 MMboe. The Jordan export optionality is a long-term strategic upside.
